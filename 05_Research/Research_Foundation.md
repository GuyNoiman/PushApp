# Research Foundation

Status: Evidence base for the PushApp investor narrative
Prepared: July 2026

## Purpose

This document grounds PushApp's core product claims in real, peer-reviewed research so the
pitch rests on evidence rather than intuition. Each pillar lists: the claim, the supporting
research, a confidence level, and — honestly — what is **not yet proven** and must be validated
by PushApp itself.

> Rule applied here: prefer rigorous meta-analyses and foundational theory over popular but
> weak citations. The frequently-quoted "Gail Matthews goal-writing study" and the
> "ASTD 65%/95% accountability appointment" statistic were **deliberately excluded** because
> they are methodologically weak and routinely misquoted in pitch decks.

## Pillar 1 — The problem is the intention–action gap, not lack of desire

**Claim:** People rarely fail for lack of ambition; they fail to convert intention into sustained action.

**Evidence:**
- **Sheeran & Webb (2016), "The Intention–Behavior Gap,"** *Social and Personality Psychology
  Compass* 10(9), 503–518. A meta-analysis of experiments found that a **medium-to-large change
  in intentions produced only a small-to-medium change in behavior** — i.e., intending is a weak
  predictor of doing. DOI: 10.1111/spc3.12265
- **Confidence: High.** This is the empirical backbone of the entire pitch.

**What PushApp still must prove:** that *its* interventions close this specific gap better than existing tools.

## Pillar 2 — Specific, committed goals outperform vague ones

**Claim:** A structured "Journey" (specific outcome + timeframe) beats a loose aspiration.

**Evidence:**
- **Locke & Latham, Goal-Setting Theory** (1990; 2002 retrospective, *American Psychologist*).
  Specific and difficult goals reliably outperform "do your best"; goal-setting is among the most
  robust findings in behavioral science, with meta-analytic effect sizes roughly **d = 0.42–0.80**.
- **Confidence: High.** Directly supports the Journey object and "commit" step.

---

## Pillar 3 — The *when/where/how* plan is what actually drives follow-through

**Claim:** Structuring the moment of action — not just the goal — is what moves the needle.

**Evidence:**
- **Gollwitzer & Sheeran (2006), "Implementation Intentions and Goal Achievement: A Meta-Analysis
  of Effects and Processes,"** *Advances in Experimental Social Psychology* 38, 69–119.
  Across **94 independent tests (8,000+ participants)**, forming an implementation intention
  ("if situation Y, then I'll do X") produced a **mean effect of d = 0.65** on goal attainment
  over holding a goal alone.
- **Confidence: High.** This is the academic license for PushApp's Journey configuration and
  context-triggered interventions.

---

## Pillar 4 — Interventions should be timely and contextual, not fixed reminders (the "why now")

**Claim:** Modern phones can deliver support at the right moment and in the right state — the core of the Intervention Engine.

**Evidence:**
- **Nahum-Shani et al. (2018), "Just-in-Time Adaptive Interventions (JITAIs) in Mobile Health:
  Key Components and Design Principles,"** *Annals of Behavioral Medicine* 52(6), 446–462.
  Establishes the design framework for delivering the right support, at the right time, adapting
  to a person's changing context and receptivity.
- **Confidence: Medium.** The *framework* is well-established and credible. **Honest caveat:**
  recent systematic reviews (e.g., *Frontiers in Digital Health*, 2025; *British Journal of Health
  Psychology*, 2025) find the *efficacy* evidence for JITAIs is still emerging and unevenly
  substantiated. This is an opportunity, not a settled result — and it is exactly what PushApp's
  data flywheel is positioned to prove.

## Pillar 5 — Human support materially improves adherence

**Claim:** Structured social support (Allies / Support Circle) increases follow-through.

**Evidence:**
- **Mohr, Cuijpers & Lehman (2011), "Supportive Accountability: A Model for Providing Human
  Support to Enhance Adherence to eHealth Interventions,"** *Journal of Medical Internet Research*
  13(1):e30. Human accountability to a trusted, benevolent other significantly improves engagement
  with digital behavior-change tools.
- **Confidence: Medium-High** for the direction of the effect.

## Pillar 6 — Identity, not tasks, is the deepest motivator

**Claim:** "You are not a finished version of yourself" is not just poetry — identity framing changes behavior.

**Evidence:**
- **Oyserman, Identity-Based Motivation (IBM) theory** (e.g., 2009, *Journal of Consumer
  Psychology*; 2015 review). When an action feels identity-congruent, difficulty is interpreted as
  *meaningful*; when it feels identity-incongruent, the same difficulty signals "not for people
  like me" and people quit. IBM has been used as the basis for real aspiration–achievement gap
  interventions in health, education, and savings.
- **Confidence: Medium-High** as a theoretical foundation for identity-first framing.

---

## Pillar 7 — Consistency beats perfection; a missed day is not failure

**Claim:** "Progress over perfection" is evidence-based, not just kind.

**Evidence:**
- **Lally et al. (2010), "How are habits formed: Modelling habit formation in the real world,"**
  *European Journal of Social Psychology* 40(6), 998–1009. Median time to reach ~95% automaticity
  was **66 days**, with a very wide range (**18–254 days**) — and critically, **missing a single
  opportunity did not materially impair habit formation.** (This also debunks the "21-day habit" myth.)
- **Confidence: High.** Directly supports the Recovery / anti-shame product principle.
